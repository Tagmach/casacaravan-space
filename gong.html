How the gong is made

Tağmaç makes gongs by upcycling — taking discarded, salvaged metal and giving it a new voice. The metal is shaped, worked and tuned by hand until it holds a note: a fundamental with a cloud of overtones around it. Every gong is different; what the metal once was, and how it's worked, decides what it will say.

That's why these recordings aren't interchangeable with a sample pack. Each one is a particular instrument — something discarded, reworked by the person striking it, recorded in the room it was tuned in. When you hear a long shimmer that never quite resolves, that's the overtones beating against each other — the thing a gong does that almost nothing else does.

About the gong
What is a gong bath?
A session where a gong (and often other instruments) is played continuously while you rest. The sound is rich and enveloping, and many people find it deeply relaxing. It's a wellness and contemplative practice — not a medical treatment, and we don't make health claims for it.
Can I use these recordings for meditation or sleep?
Yes — that's what the seamless loops are for. They're designed to run for a long time without breaks. Use them however helps you: meditation, focus, winding down. Volume low is usually plenty.
What's the difference between a recording, a loop, and a stem?
A recording is a single gong sounding, captured as-is. A loop is a long, seamless version made to run continuously. A stem is a clean, isolated sound licensed for you to build into your own work.
Where can I hear a gong live?
Tağmaç runs Sonic Journey sound sessions in Lefkoşa, Cyprus — in person and online. A gong bath in the room is a different thing from headphones; the vibration is physical.
