About

A space that helps
you realize yourself.

It was 2015, somewhere in Guatemala, searching for mucuna pruriens with a companion. We decided to go to Tikal — and since my friend knew the area, he said: let's enter from the village behind, through Uaxactún. We would spend the night there and walk into Tikal the next morning.

We arrived in the afternoon. A village in the middle of a vast jungle — 28 square kilometers shared with Belize. A doctor joined us along the way. My companion had brought his flute. The doctor had brought her machete.

There were Mayan ruins in the forest behind the village. Let's go, someone said. We walked a trail into the trees for maybe half an hour. Remnants along the way — ancient walls, burial mounds. We kept walking until we arrived at the central plaza of an ancient settlement. Pyramids everywhere.

We climbed one. Sat at the top. My companion played the flute. I danced with the doctor's machete. Above us, a canopy of enormous trees. Monkeys singing and moving through the branches.

Then I separated from the group and wandered to the residential area behind the pyramid. A guardian came toward me with his rifle. We talked — my broken Spanish, his patience. He showed me the two-room house where the community gathered, and the trees with notches cut into the bark, where tribe members climbed to sleep in the canopy at night.

On the way back, I was still barefoot. "There are poisonous insects here," the guardian warned. I put on my shoes.

When I returned to where I had left my friends, the sky had darkened. They were gone. And I realized I didn't remember the way back.

I tried to ask the guardian which path led to the village. He pointed to two trails and said something I couldn't understand. I remembered the size of this jungle. I took a deep breath, turned on my phone's flashlight, and walked in.

I was frightened. The light only showed what was directly ahead — everything else was absolute darkness. The light felt wrong. Not just for me. I was disturbing the forest too.

So I turned it off. I closed my eyes and waited — to let my night vision return. I waited. And I prayed that the moonlight would show me the path.

When I opened my eyes, it had. I could see the trail. I could see the forest. I had become part of it.

And then, one by one, fireflies began to appear. They blinked at the edges of the path, calling me deeper into the forest. I thought about following them. But I knew — today, I needed to arrive somewhere.

I kept walking. And when I saw the red tree I had noticed at the village entrance, I knew I had made it.

Casa Caravan started as a physical space in Northern Cyprus — a real place, a real community. Sourdough bread that stopped for one week after the pandemic, then never stopped again. Ukiyo — goji, cashew, coconut butter — the first product that truly landed. A name that means floating world.

The community chose sleep. Geography became a wall. So we turned off the flashlight. Closed our eyes. And when we opened them — this space appeared.

casacaravan.space is the digital life of the same hands, the same philosophy, the same frequency. A house with rooms to work in. A garden that changes with the seasons. A forest that begins where the known ends.

The fireflies are still there. You just have to turn off the light to see them.
